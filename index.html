Deposits & No-Show Protection

Designing financial protection for independent beauty professionals. The feature had to work cleanly for dozens of scenarios across both sides of the marketplace, from simple bookings to complex cancellation edge cases.

Role
Lead Product Designer
Platform
Web & Mobile
Launched
June 2024
Team
PM, Engineering, Research

Background

No-shows were costing pros real money with no recourse.

Independent beauty professionals on StyleSeat run tight schedules where every appointment slot has real financial value. A no-show doesn't just mean lost revenue. It means lost time that can't be recovered, often with no notice and no way to hold the client accountable.

Deposits were the standard protection offline, but StyleSeat had nothing in place digitally. Building this meant designing an experience that worked for both sides of the marketplace: pros needed control and confidence, clients needed transparency and trust.

My Role

End-to-end design across both sides of the transaction.

I owned the full design of this feature: pro-facing settings and setup, client-facing booking and checkout, and the extensive set of edge cases that come with a financial transaction on a two-sided marketplace. This wasn't just a single flow. It touched nearly every surface of the platform.

Key Design Decisions

The hard problems I had to solve.

01

Deposit Customization Settings

Designed a settings experience giving pros full control over how deposits worked for their business: how much to charge, which services required a deposit, and who had to pay one. Options included requiring deposits from all clients, new clients only, or exempting specific trusted clients from the VIP list.

02

VIP Client Exemption List

Pros with long-term trusted clients needed a way to exempt them from deposit requirements without losing the protection for everyone else. I designed an experience where pros could browse their full client list and designate specific clients as VIPs, giving those clients a deposit-free booking experience while keeping the protection in place for new and unknown clients.

03

Cancellation Edge Cases

This was the most complex design challenge on the feature. The refund logic had to account for who cancelled, when they cancelled, and what the pro's policy was, which resulted in very different outcomes. A client who cancels within the window loses the deposit. A pro who cancels owes a full refund. A New Client Request that the pro declines needed its own logic entirely, since the pro never accepted the booking in the first place. I mapped and designed every permutation of this to ensure the experience was clear, fair, and consistent for both parties.

04

Client-Facing Transparency

Deposits can feel like a red flag to clients who aren't expecting them. I designed the client booking experience to surface the deposit requirement early, with a clear breakdown of what was due at booking versus at the appointment. This information carried through to all client communications including confirmation emails, reminders, and the appointment detail screen, so there were no surprises.

05

Beta Research and Validation

Before full launch, we ran a structured beta with real pros. Key findings: 70% reported fewer cancellations, 62% saw fewer no-shows, and 76% rated their overall experience Above Average or Excellent. I used this feedback to refine the onboarding flow, policy language, and the checkout experience before going wide.

Solution

A flexible, two-sided deposit system that worked for every scenario.

The final product was a fully customizable deposit system that pros could configure to fit their specific business. From a dedicated settings screen, pros could set a deposit amount, choose which services required one, and define who had to pay: all clients, new clients only, or everyone except their curated VIP list.

The VIP list became its own mini-feature: a searchable view of the pro's full client history where they could designate trusted regulars as exempt from the deposit requirement without manually managing exceptions every time.

On the client side, the deposit was surfaced clearly and early in the booking flow with a plain-language explanation and a breakdown of what was due at booking versus at the appointment. Every client communication, including confirmation, reminder, and appointment detail, reinforced this so there were no surprises at checkout.

Cancellation logic was handled through a clear policy framework the pro set upfront. Clients who cancelled within the window forfeited the deposit. Pros who cancelled issued a full refund automatically. New Client Requests that a pro declined triggered an immediate refund with no action required from the client. Every edge case had a defined, fair resolution the system handled without manual intervention.

AI Social Post Builder

The first AI content creation tool in the beauty industry. No competitors, no playbook. I designed the entire experience from scratch, including the AI prompting strategy, topic architecture, and brand personalization system.

Lead Product Designer
Platform
Web & Mobile
Type
0 to 1 Feature
Team

Background

No playbook, no competitors, no precedent.

When this project started, no one in the beauty industry had built an AI social media content tool for independent professionals. There was nothing to reference, nothing to benchmark against. This was a true zero-to-one problem.

Pros knew social media was important for their business but consistently said it was one of the most stressful parts of running their practice. The issue wasn't motivation. It was the blank canvas. They didn't know what to post, how to make it look professional, or how to keep it consistent with their brand.

The Core Challenge

Getting AI to produce results pros would actually trust.

The biggest design challenge on this project wasn't the UI. It was figuring out how to make AI output good enough that pros would feel confident sharing it. Generic, sloppy, or off-brand posts wouldn't just go unused. They could actively hurt a pro's reputation and StyleSeat's brand.

We needed a system where the inputs were controlled enough to produce consistently good results, but flexible enough that posts didn't feel identical across thousands of different pros and businesses.

Key Design Decisions

How I solved it.

01

Topic Category Architecture

Rather than giving pros an open text field to enter their own prompts, I designed a curated set of topic categories as the primary input. Categories like client reviews, open appointments, seasonal promotions, and service spotlights gave the AI enough context to produce relevant, on-topic content while keeping the experience simple for pros who weren't content creators. I worked closely with the PM and marketing team on category selection and the prompt language behind each one.

02

AI Prompt Workshopping

I ran structured sessions testing multiple AI models and prompting strategies to find the combination that produced the best results across different topic types and business styles. This wasn't just a technical exercise. The output quality directly shaped how the product was designed. Seeing what the AI could and couldn't do reliably informed every decision about what to put in the UI and what to keep out.

03

The MVP Scope Decision

Early on we considered letting pros write their own custom AI prompts to generate posts outside our topic system. We pulled this from MVP for two reasons: quality and brand risk. Without controlled inputs, the outputs could be unpredictable, and a poorly generated post shared from a StyleSeat pro's account reflects on the platform, not just the pro. We shipped topic-based generation first to validate the concept with quality guardrails in place before investing in open-ended prompting.

04

Brand Personalization System

To solve the "everyone's posts look the same" problem, I designed a brand personalization layer where pros could select their brand colors and choose an overall visual style. This meant the AI output wasn't just different by topic. It reflected each pro's individual aesthetic. A high-contrast editorial style for one stylist, a soft warm tone for another. The posts felt personal, not templated.

05

Edit, Download, and Share Flow

Designed the full downstream experience: pros could review the generated post, make edits to the copy, download the image, and share directly to Instagram or save for later. The editing step was intentional. It gave pros ownership over the final result and reduced the friction of trusting AI output they hadn't written themselves.

Solution

A structured AI content tool with brand personalization built in.

The Social Post Builder launched as a topic-based content generator. Pros chose from a curated set of categories including client reviews, open appointments, seasonal promotions, and service spotlights, and the tool used those inputs along with the pro's profile data to generate a ready-to-share social post.

Before generating, pros configured their brand profile by selecting their brand colors and choosing an overall visual style. This meant every post felt like it came from that specific business rather than a generic template shared across thousands of providers. A high-contrast editorial look for one stylist, a warm and personal tone for another.

After generation, pros could review the copy, make edits directly in the tool, preview the finished post, and download or share to Instagram with one tap. The edit step was a deliberate design decision. Giving pros ownership over the final result made the AI output feel like a starting point they controlled, not something they had to trust blindly.

Custom AI prompting was intentionally left out of the MVP. The topic system gave us quality control and brand safety from day one while we validated that the core concept worked. Expanding to open-ended prompting became a future roadmap item, not a launch requirement.

Adoption and a strong quality signal.

Custom Websites

A top-requested feature that pros couldn't afford or build on their own. I designed the entire product from zero: three professional templates, a no-code builder, and a hero image system, all built to get a pro live in minutes, not hours.

Role
Lead Product Designer
Platform
Web & Mobile
Launched
August 2025
Team
PM, Engineering, Marketing

Background

A top request pros couldn't afford to build themselves.

Independent beauty professionals consistently asked for a way to have their own website. The problem was that building one was expensive, time-consuming, and required technical skills most pros didn't have. Third-party website builders were an option, but they were disconnected from the StyleSeat booking system and required starting from scratch with no industry-specific templates.

This feature didn't exist anywhere on the platform before I started working on it. There was no prior version to iterate on. Just a strong signal from pros that it was something they needed.

My Role

Two parallel design problems.

This project had two distinct design challenges that had to work together. The first was the builder experience, the tool pros use to create and customize their site. The second was the templates themselves, the actual websites that would represent a pro's business to potential clients.

I owned both. The builder had to be simple enough that a non-technical pro could go from sign-up to live site in a single session. The templates had to look professional enough that pros felt proud sharing them, and client-focused enough to actually drive bookings.

Key Design Decisions

How I solved it.

01

Template Design from Scratch

I designed three professional website templates from zero. Each one had to work for a wide range of beauty businesses, from solo nail techs to high-end hair salons, while still feeling distinct enough that pros could choose the one that matched their aesthetic. Templates were built to automatically pull in data from the pro's existing StyleSeat profile, so the site felt personalized from the moment they selected a template.

02

Hero Image System

The hero image is the most important visual element on any website. I designed a three-option system: pros could use a curated default image we provided, choose from a StyleSeat photo library we built specifically for this feature with high-quality beauty industry photography, or upload their own photo. Building the curated library required sourcing and organizing a collection of quality images that represented the range of beauty professionals on the platform.

03

Simple Onboarding Flow

The entire setup experience was designed to take minutes, not hours. Pros selected a template, customized their brand colors, chose a hero image, wrote a headline, picked a light or dark theme, and previewed the result before publishing. Six steps. No code, no complexity, no decisions that required technical knowledge. The site was automatically populated with their services, bio, and booking link from their existing StyleSeat profile.

04

Paywall and Monetization Design

After an extended free trial period, Custom Websites moved to a $10/month add-on to a pro's existing StyleSeat subscription. I designed the paywall experience to feel like a natural next step rather than an interruption. Pros had already seen the value of their site during the trial period, so the conversion moment was designed around reinforcing that value rather than creating urgency.

Solution

Three professional templates. A six-step builder. Live in minutes.

The Custom Websites product launched with three professionally designed templates, each built from scratch to look credible for a range of beauty businesses, from solo practitioners to established salons. Templates automatically pulled in the pro's existing StyleSeat profile data including their services, bio, and booking link, so the site felt personalized from the moment they selected a template.

The builder experience was designed around a single constraint: get a pro to a live, professional-looking site before they finished their morning coffee. The flow had six steps: select a template, set brand colors, choose a hero image, write a headline, pick a light or dark theme, preview, and publish. No technical knowledge required at any step.

The hero image system gave pros three options: a curated default image, a StyleSeat photo library we built specifically for this feature with high-quality beauty industry photography, or their own uploaded photo. Building the curated library was itself a design and curation effort, sourcing and organizing images that represented the real range of pros on the platform.

After an extended free trial, the feature moved to a $10/month add-on. The paywall was designed to feel like a natural next step for pros who had already seen their site live during the trial, reinforcing the value they'd already experienced rather than creating pressure to convert.

Medicare Shopping Experience

Simplifying the hardest parts of Medicare shopping for seniors on mobile. I focused on plan comparison, plan details, search results, and the drug lookup tool, applying deep health insurance domain knowledge to problems that most designers would struggle to even frame correctly.

Lead UX/UI Designer
Company

Timeline
Jan – Oct 2021
Team

Background

Medicare is genuinely hard to shop for. We had to make it feel easy.

HealthInsurance.com was rebuilding its Medicare shopping experience to coincide with a company rebrand and a move to a modern tech stack. The existing site had usability issues, accessibility failures, and a design that didn't reflect the new brand. The deadline was hard: Medicare open enrollment.

I came into this project with years of health insurance design experience. I understood the difference between a deductible and an out-of-pocket maximum, how drug formularies worked, and why a senior with a specific prescription might choose one plan over another even if it had a higher premium. That domain knowledge shaped every design decision I made.

My Role

Plan details, comparison, results, and the drug lookup tool.

Within a larger team working on the full experience, I was primarily responsible for the plan detail pages, plan comparison tool, search results and filters, and the prescription drug lookup. I also contributed to the "Help Me Choose" guided recommendation tool, specifically around which questions to ask and how to present the plan summary at the end.

These were the hardest parts of the product to design: where information density was highest and the stakes for getting it wrong were greatest.

Key Design Decisions

Where my work made the biggest difference.

01

Simplifying the Drug Lookup Tool

The original drug lookup was a complex form with too many input fields presented all at once. For a senior who just wanted to know if their prescriptions were covered, it was overwhelming. I redesigned it as a guided sequential experience, one input per screen in a step-by-step flow. Enter a drug name, confirm the dosage, select the form, confirm the frequency. Each step was clear and low-pressure, and the result was a much higher completion rate and less confusion about what information was being asked for.

02

Plan Comparison on Mobile

Comparing Medicare plans on a mobile screen is a genuinely hard layout problem. Plans have dozens of attributes including premiums, deductibles, copays, drug coverage, and star ratings, and the default side-by-side table layout that works on desktop falls apart on a narrow phone screen. I redesigned the mobile comparison experience by fitting columns more efficiently and stacking the content within each plan so users could scan vertically through one plan at a time while maintaining the ability to compare across plans. The goal was making it easy to spot the key differences without getting lost in the data.

03

Plan Results and Filters

Designed the plan results page and filtering system to surface the most relevant options quickly without overwhelming users with choices. Given my insurance background, I understood which filters seniors actually used to narrow their decision: premium range, drug coverage, star rating, and provider network, and designed the filter architecture around those priorities rather than exposing every possible attribute.

04

Help Me Choose: Questions and Plan Summary

I contributed to the "Help Me Choose" guided tool by helping define the question set and the prescription drug selection step specifically. I also designed the plan summary screen shown at the end of the guided flow, a simplified overview that translated the plan's key details into plain language relevant to the answers the user had just given, rather than showing the full plan data set.

05

Research Influence: Guided vs. Browse

Weekly user research sessions run throughout the project produced a key finding that shaped the entire experience: 90% of users preferred to be guided to a plan recommendation rather than browse on their own. This validated the investment in the "Help Me Choose" tool and influenced how prominently we featured the guided path as the primary entry point to the shopping experience.

Solution

Simpler inputs, better comparisons, clearer plan recommendations.

The drug lookup shipped as a sequential guided experience, one input per screen. Users entered a drug name, confirmed the dosage, selected the form, and confirmed the frequency across four clean, focused steps rather than a single overwhelming form. Completion rates improved and user confusion dropped significantly.

The mobile plan comparison was redesigned to make scanning across plans actually manageable on a narrow screen. By fitting columns more efficiently and stacking content within each plan vertically, users could compare key attributes across multiple plans without horizontal scrolling or losing their place in the data.

Plan results were organized around the filters seniors actually used to narrow their decisions: premium range, drug coverage, star rating, and provider network, rather than exposing every possible plan attribute upfront. Plan detail pages translated insurance terminology into plain language wherever possible, reducing the cognitive load of evaluating a plan.

The "Help Me Choose" guided tool gave users who didn't want to browse an alternative entry point. The question set I contributed to included a prescription drug step that connected directly to the drug lookup tool, so the plan recommendation at the end reflected the user's actual medication needs. The plan summary shown at the end of the guided flow was a simplified overview in plain language, not a data dump of every plan attribute.

Launched on time. Outperformed prior year.
